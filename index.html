About this scale

The survey on YourMorals.org is a combination of the Empathizing and Systemizing scales, created by Simon Baron-Cohen at Cambridge University. The official version uses 20 items from each scale.

The scale measures two abilities or preferences on which human minds differ. Empathizing is “the drive to identify another person’s emotions and thoughts, and to respond to these with an appropriate emotion.” Systemizing is “the drive to analyze the variables in a system, and to derive the underlying rules that govern the behavior of the system.” In short, empathizing is about understanding the social world whereas systemizing is about understanding the world of inanimate objects and nature.

Why this matters

Baron-Cohen has found large sex differences on both traits. Men tend to score higher than women on systemizing and lower than women on empathizing. But Baron-Cohen’s main interest is not sex differences — it is autism. He has found that people with mild autism or Asperger’s syndrome seem to have a kind of “extreme male brain.” They love mechanical and classificatory systems and find it very difficult to get inside people’s heads and understand emotional reactions.

The YourMorals version is interesting for political psychology because there are two competing stereotypes about liberals and conservatives. On one hand, conservatives are thought to be a bit more “macho” or masculine than liberals (at least for men), but on the other hand, many political theorists have linked liberalism with a tendency to abstraction and to thinking about society as a system that can be managed and optimized. We therefore can’t predict whether liberals or conservatives will show the more “male” pattern of lower empathy and higher systemizing. Or might liberals score higher on both empathizing and systemizing? Will the story be different for men and women? Let’s see!

Scoring

Scores for systemizing range from 1 to 4, and higher numbers indicate a higher degree of systemizing. Scores for empathizing also range from 1 to 4, and higher numbers indicate a higher degree of empathizing.

To learn more about systemizing, empathizing, and the scales, see this article by Daniel Nettle and the Wikipedia page on Simon Baron-Cohen and his research.
